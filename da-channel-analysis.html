Where you actually stand — context first

Visual Identity

Your thumbnail design — what I actually see

Looking across all three screenshots, you have a clearer visual identity than most channels at this stage, which is to your credit. You are not all over the place. But there are several patterns that are limiting your CTR potential, and they are consistent enough to address systematically.

Your dominant thumbnail formula right now: one face (usually Agnes, occasionally Dina) positioned either left or right, large bold text in green or white overlaid on a dark background, CapCut X logo present, and relatively clean composition. The green text and green accent colour is becoming recognisable as a brand signature, which is good. The problem is the execution is inconsistent in ways that hurt legibility and click-through.

What is working in your thumbnails

  • Faces are present and correctly sized. Most of your thumbnails include a face at reasonable scale. This puts you ahead of the "UI screenshot only" trap that kills many tutorial channels.
  • Green accent colour is becoming a brand signature. The bright green on dark is appearing consistently enough across thumbnails that it is starting to build visual identity. This is valuable — do not abandon it.
  • Bold, large text in most thumbnails. The text-to-image ratio is generally good — text is dominant enough to communicate the hook at small sizes.
  • Topic focus is clear. Nearly every thumbnail communicates CapCut + a specific skill or technique. No confusion about what the channel is about.
  • Expressions have improved in recent videos. Comparing screenshot 3 (oldest) to screenshot 1 (newest), the faces are more engaged and energetic in recent thumbnails. The "Realistic Shadows" thumbnail with the exaggerated shock expression is noticeably more clickable than earlier flat expressions.

What is limiting your thumbnails

  • Background inconsistency is your biggest design problem. Across 38 thumbnails you have: solid black, dark room, bright green, colourful gradient (purple/teal), dark with colour wheel elements, dark with waveforms, warm orange/amber room lighting. A viewer cannot build pattern recognition from this. Matt Loui and Patrik Key both have a dominant background type that repeats — you don't yet. Pick one and commit.
  • Text colour is inconsistent. You use: white, green, red, yellow, mixed. Some thumbnails have white main text with green accent. Others have all green. Others have red and white. Others have green and white split. The inconsistency prevents a clear brand read at a glance. You need one primary text colour and one accent, used consistently.
  • Some thumbnails have weak or absent expressions. Several thumbnails show a relatively neutral or slightly smiling face — not the high-energy surprised/excited/knowing expression that drives clicks in this niche. The "5 Text Animations" thumbnail (Agnes holding the CapCut logo, calm expression) is an example. Compare to the "Realistic Shadows" thumbnail which has a far more engaging expression.
  • Text sometimes competes with background elements. On thumbnails with complex backgrounds (colour wheels, waveforms, colour grading UI) the text can become difficult to read quickly. Dark or blurred backgrounds work better because they give the text room to breathe.
  • The dual-presenter opportunity is not being used. Having two people is genuinely distinctive in this space. But your thumbnails are single-face in almost every case. Even using both faces occasionally — especially for your higher-stakes videos — would create a visual moment that stands out on the feed.
  • Numbers in titles do not always appear in thumbnails. "PRO 30 TIPS" appears in the thumbnail for that video (good). "10 INSANE Text Animations", "4 SMART AI Features" — these have text in the thumbnail but the number isn't always the dominant visual element. The number should be the largest text element on the thumbnail for numbered videos. It is the click driver.

Immediate Actions

Your green accent colour is working and is distinctive. Keep it. But choose one background type and commit: either near-black cinematic, or dark with a single colour gradient (the dark green you've used on "3 Transitions" and a few others works well). Stick to white main text + green accent word, or green main text on dark. Pick one and use it on every video from now. This is the single highest-leverage design decision you can make right now — it costs nothing to implement and starts compounding immediately.
