The Right Frame

How to read your numbers at this stage

A channel with 460 subscribers does not get the same starting velocity as a channel with 100K subscribers. When Matt Loui publishes a video, over 100,000 people get a notification. When you publish, roughly 460 do — and not all of those will open it in the first 48 hours. That means your first-day view counts are structurally different, and comparing them directly is meaningless. What matters at your stage is different: are people who find your videos watching them? Are those viewers becoming subscribers? Is the channel growing?

The VPH ratios you can see in your screenshots — 5.1x, 4.3x, 3.5x, 2.9x on your best videos — are the real signal. These ratios tell you how your video is performing relative to your own channel baseline, regardless of absolute size. A 5.1x ratio means that video is pulling in views at 5x the rate of your average. That is a strong signal regardless of whether the absolute number is 1,300 or 1,300,000.

Your 40% monthly growth rate is the most important number on your channel right now. That rate, sustained, means you compound fast. A channel that grows 40% per month goes from 460 subscribers to roughly 2,000 in 3 months, 9,000 in 6 months. The view counts that feel small today will look very different by October when your course launches — if you keep the growth rate. That is where your focus belongs.

The honest summary before anything else

You are doing well for 3–4 months in. The channel is not in trouble. It is growing faster than most channels at this stage. The analysis below is about refinements — not about fixing something broken. Several of your thumbnails have real strengths. A few specific patterns are worth adjusting. Nothing requires a dramatic overhaul.

Thumbnail Design

What your thumbnails are actually doing

Looking across all 38 thumbnails in your screenshots, the clearest observation is that your visual identity is genuinely forming. You have a recognisable palette — dark backgrounds, bold white or green text, the CapCut X logo, face forward — and it is consistent enough that someone scrolling would start to recognise a Dina & Agnes thumbnail. That is not nothing at 3–4 months in. Most channels at this age are still figuring out what they look like.

The two presenters are correctly assigned: Dina's videos have Dina, Agnes's videos have Agnes. Both are featured clearly and at good scale. Expressions have visibly improved from your oldest thumbnails to your newest — the more recent work shows more energy and engagement in the face, which is the right direction.

What is working

  • Both faces appear where relevant and are well-sized. The "YOU NEED THIS" thumbnail has both Dina and Agnes — and it's one of your more visually distinctive thumbnails because of that energy dynamic between two faces pointing at something together.
  • Green as a brand colour is working. The bright green accent on dark — "3 Transitions that look clean," "PRO 30 TIPS," the green backgrounds on several thumbnails — is becoming a recognisable signature. This is good. Protect it and make it consistent.
  • Text scale is generally appropriate. Most thumbnails have text that reads clearly at thumbnail size. You are not making the common mistake of small decorative text that disappears at 120px.
  • After Effects comparison framing is in several thumbnails ("Text Animations That Look Like AE", "Animate Logos Like After Effects") and those videos are among your better performers. The framing is visually signalled in both the title and the thumbnail in those cases — that alignment is what makes it work.
  • Expression energy is improving over time. Your newer thumbnails (screenshot 1) show more animated, engaged expressions compared to the earliest work (screenshot 3). This is the right direction and it's happening naturally as you get more comfortable on camera.

What to refine

  • Background type changes too often. Across 38 thumbnails you have: plain black, dark room with studio lighting, bright solid green, colourful gradient, dark with colour wheel graphics, dark with audio waveforms, warm amber room. This variety prevents viewers from building instant pattern recognition. You do not need to pick one forever — but settling on two or three background types that rotate would build a more cohesive grid feel on your channel page.
  • Text colour system is not yet locked. Some thumbnails use white text with green accent word. Some use all-green text. Some use red and white. Some mix green, white and yellow. Picking one system — for example, always white as the primary text colour with one green accent word — would tighten the brand significantly without changing the overall feel.
  • The CapCut logo features very heavily across thumbnails. It appears in the vast majority of your thumbnails, often as a centrepiece element. The logo is useful for category recognition, especially while the channel is small. But on technique and effect videos, the visual result of the technique is a more clickable centrepiece than the logo. Agnes holding up the CapCut logo is fine — Agnes reacting to the text animation effect in frame would be stronger. The logo can move to a corner or smaller supporting position on those videos.
  • On numbered videos, the number itself should dominate. Your best numbered thumbnail is "PRO 30 TIPS" where "30" is large and immediately readable. Some other numbered videos have the number as one of several text elements of similar weight — "4 INSANE", "10 INSANE", "3 Color Tricks." The number should be visually larger than everything else on the thumbnail. That size signals value density before the viewer reads a single word.
  • Title energy and thumbnail expression sometimes diverge. The "4 INSANE CapCut Tricks" thumbnail shows Agnes smiling and holding up the logo — which reads as warm and approachable. The word INSANE in the title implies something more surprising or jaw-dropping. Neither is wrong, but aligning them — either use a different title word or use a more exaggerated expression — would make the package feel more consistent. This is a minor refinement, not a major problem.
On the "Every Editor Needs This" video — 95 views

This video has both your faces in the thumbnail, clear text, and is well-composed. The low view count is not a thumbnail problem — it is a content type problem. This is effectively a product promotion video for your Creator Asset Pack, and audiences can sense that even before clicking. The title gives it away: "Every Editor Needs This | Motion Graphics, SFX, LUTs And More" reads as a commercial, not a tutorial. People come to your channel to learn how to do something. A product promotion, however well packaged, will always underperform tutorial content on a tutorial channel. The fix isn't the thumbnail — it's either accepting that promo videos will have lower views, or reframing the content as a tutorial that naturally features the pack (e.g., "I built a cinematic edit using only assets from my pack — here's how").

From Competitor Research

What to extract from the competitor data — applied to your stage

The competitor channels are not a comparison point for view counts. They are a research source. Here is what their data tells you that is directly applicable to a channel at your size and stage — framed as patterns to adopt, not as benchmarks to hit.

Formats proven to drive above-average performance — adopt these
  • Specific numbered lists with the number dominant in the thumbnail. All three competitors confirm this. Your own channel confirms it (30 Tips, 3 Transitions, 5 Text Animations). The number needs to be the largest element.
  • After Effects comparison framing. Appears in Matt Loui's 176k video, Patrik Key's positioning, multiple Joey Edits videos, and confirmed in your own "5 Text Animations Like AE" at 2.9x. This format has proven search demand and your audience responds to it.
  • Curiosity-gap hooks. "You Won't Believe," "Pros Do This," "Nobody Teaches This," "It Feels Illegal." Your own "You Won't Believe This Was Made in CapCut" is your highest VPH video right now. The hook format works at any channel size because it generates clicks from the feed, not just from subscribers.
  • Question-format titles. "Your Text Looks Boring?" is one of your better performers. The direct question format creates identification — viewers self-select in if it describes their problem. Underused across your channel right now.
  • AI + CapCut pairing. Joey Edits' 14.3x ratio on this format is not a coincidence. The search demand for AI workflow content is rising and not yet saturated in the CapCut space. This is a time-sensitive opportunity.
Patterns to avoid — confirmed by competitor data and your own
  • Vague "tricks/tips that make a difference" titles without saying what the difference is. Specific skill titles consistently outperform across all three competitor channels and on yours.
  • Product promotion framing. Low across all three competitors too. Tutorial audience does not respond to feeling like they're being sold to.
  • Off-niche content. Matt Loui's MacBook and Filmora videos consistently underperform. Stay in your lane — CapCut desktop tutorials. Your audience came for that and only that.
  • Complex backgrounds competing with text. Consistent finding across all three channels and yours. Simpler backgrounds perform better because they give text and face room to breathe.
