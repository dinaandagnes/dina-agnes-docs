Overview

What the data actually shows

Having looked through all three channels properly, the picture is more nuanced than any generalisation suggests. Each creator has a distinct visual identity, a distinct content strategy, and distinct performance patterns. The one thing that is genuinely universal: face-forward thumbnails with large, legible text consistently outperform no-face or UI-screenshot-only thumbnails across all three channels without exception.

Beyond that, the patterns diverge significantly. Matt Loui is the most evolved and most inconsistent — his channel has gone through multiple visual identities and his highest-performing work sits in very specific formats. Joey Edits is the most systematically formatted creator of the three, with the clearest repeatable thumbnail template. Patrik Key is the smallest but arguably the sharpest strategically — his positioning is tighter and his recent thumbnails show a creator finding a distinct visual voice.

The single most important number in this dataset: Matt Loui's "15 BEST CapCut Tricks — Noob to PRO" has 1.6 million views and a 79.2x ratio. That is a category-defining outlier and the clearest content signal in all three channels combined. The format that produced it is directly replicable.

Thumbnail visual identity — what I actually see

Matt Loui does not have one consistent thumbnail formula — he has evolved through several, and you can trace this across the screenshots chronologically. His oldest content (2–3 years ago) used almost no face, relying entirely on text-over-footage or software UI screenshots. Performance on those is uniformly poor — most sitting under 30k views. His middle-era work (1–2 years ago) introduced his face alongside bold kinetic typography on dark backgrounds. His current thumbnails lean more heavily on face expression combined with one large text hook and frequent use of the CapCut logo as a visual anchor.

The most consistent visual traits across his current work: dark or black backgrounds with his face slightly off-centre, white bold text with a single colour accent word, and the CapCut X logo placed prominently. He regularly uses a muted shocked/incredulous expression rather than an aggressive high-energy face — more "you won't believe this" than "this is amazing." His backgrounds are rarely clean — he often composites UI elements, app icons or abstract graphics behind or beside his face.

One important observation: his most recent thumbnails (last 1–2 months, image 1) show him experimenting more aggressively — the Seedance 2.0 thumbnail uses animated AI visuals, the "Pros do this" thumbnail is cleaner and text-forward. This suggests he is actively A/B testing formats right now, which means his formula is not fixed.

What works in his thumbnails

  • High text legibility — bold, white or yellow, readable at small sizes
  • Consistent use of CapCut X logo builds category recognition
  • Dark background creates contrast that makes face and text pop
  • Curiosity-gap text hooks — "Pros do this", "never do this", "you won't believe"
  • Rainbow/colourful border accent on some thumbnails creates brand distinctiveness
What is weak or inconsistent
  • No single locked formula — visual identity shifts video to video
  • Some thumbnails overcrowded — too many icons, text blocks and graphics competing

  • Occasional face expressions are flat — not high-energy enough to earn the click

Thumbnail visual identity — what I actually see

Patrik Key has the most visually consistent thumbnail formula of the three creators, which is striking given he appears to be the smallest channel. He has clearly reverse-engineered what works and locked it in. His formula is almost identical across every thumbnail: his face in close-up on the right side, near-expressionless with cold direct eye contact, a dark near-black background, and a bold text statement or visual graphic on the left. He uses a bald head with sharp facial features that photograph well in close-up, and he leans into that deliberately — the thumbnails are cold, serious, professional.

What is visually interesting about Patrik's thumbnails is the text treatment. He uses large, clear typography with deliberate colour contrast — white main text, gold or yellow accent words, and occasionally a bright graphic element (green glowing charts, money visualisations, app icons). He consistently shows value-related visual cues: dollar amounts, client notification mock-ups, income graphs. This is directly tied to his "CapCut to clients" positioning and it works as a visual differentiator — no other creator in this dataset uses money/income imagery in thumbnails this consistently.

His older thumbnails (10+ months ago) used a bright neon-green and black palette with more hyperactive expression, which performed less well. He has clearly evolved toward the colder, more authoritative look and it appears to be working better based on the VPH data visible in the recent thumbnails.

What works in his thumbnails

  • Income/money visual cues are unique in this space and appeal to the ambitious creator

Thumbnail visual identity — what I actually see

Joey Edits is the most prolific creator of the three by far, and the volume shows in his thumbnail approach — he runs multiple concurrent template series. Looking across all 10 screenshots, I can identify at least four distinct repeating thumbnail formats he cycles between.

Format 1 — "Edit Like [Famous Creator]": Red banner with white text top-left, the target creator's face on the right with a white arrow pointing to them, his own face on the left. This is used repeatedly across videos featuring Iman Gadzhi, MagnatesMedia, Natt Jongsala, Vox, Isaac, Ali Abdaal. It is his clearest branded template and it generates strong search pull from fans of those creators.

Format 2 — "Yes You Can" series: Red pill/banner with white text bottom-left, his face or a guest creator's face alongside the CapCut logo and an arrow. Used across multiple "Can I edit like X in CapCut PC?" videos. Visually consistent and recognisable.

Format 3 — Bold text + face: Large all-caps white or yellow text dominating most of the thumbnail space, his face occupying the remaining side. Used for tips, tricks and compilation content. Clean and punchy.

Format 4 — No-face / graphic only: Shows the actual editing technique, app icons, before/after comparison, or CapCut interface. Used less in recent work, more in early work. These are his consistently weaker performers.

Visually, Joey's thumbnails use far more colour saturation than Matt Loui — brighter backgrounds, more contrast, more use of red and yellow. His face expressions are high-energy and warm — big smiles, wide eyes, genuine enthusiasm. This creates a different emotional tone from both Matt Loui (serious/credible) and Patrik Key (cold/authoritative).

What works in his thumbnails
  • Multiple locked template series create instant recognition for repeat viewers
  • "Edit Like [Creator]" series is a repeatable format with built-in search traffic
  • High energy, warm expressions are approachable and invite clicks from beginners
  • Bright colour palette stands out from the dark-dominant thumbnails in this space
  • Red accent banners create a consistent visual identity across the whole channel
  • Arrow devices consistently used to direct eye toward key element — clean visual grammar
  • VPH multipliers on his best content are very strong (14.3x on Claude + CapCut is impressive)
What is weak or missing
  • Runs too many series simultaneously — channel feels scattered to first-time visitors
  • Heavy PC focus limits audience to desktop editors — a smaller subset of CapCut users
  • Some thumbnails are graphically cluttered — too many visual elements competing
  • Sponsored/review content (UniConverter, Aiarty etc.) dilutes content trust
  • Off-niche content (Filmora, DaVinci, fitness video timers) confuses channel identity

Design Analysis

Thumbnail patterns that actually perform — ranked by evidence

These patterns are ranked by how consistently they correlate with high view counts and strong VPH ratios across all three channels. Not theory — what the data shows.

Rank 01 — Strongest evidence
Face + large text hook + dark or high-contrast background
The dominant pattern across all three channels' best performers. Face occupies roughly 30–50% of the frame, one clear bold text statement occupies the rest. Dark or cinematic background keeps focus on face and text. Seen in virtually every video above 100k views across all three channels. The text must be readable at 100px width — if it needs squinting it fails.
Rank 02 — Strong evidence (Joey Edits)
Locked template series — "Edit Like", "Yes You Can", etc.
Joey Edits' "Edit Like [Creator]" red-banner template is his most repeatable high-performer. The template itself becomes recognisable over time — returning viewers know what they're getting. Creates a content series that compounds. The red banner + arrow + two-face layout is visually distinctive in a way that builds channel identity fast. Patrik Key's consistent close-up formula achieves something similar.
Rank 03 — Strong evidence (Matt Loui)
The effect/technique visually shown inside the thumbnail
Matt Loui's 717k typography video and the kinetic motion thumbnails show the actual result — not just a face promising a result. When the thumbnail demonstrates the technique visually, it reduces viewer uncertainty about what they'll get. His text-effect thumbnails (where the text design IS the thumbnail design) are among his strongest. Underused by all three creators relative to how well it performs when they do it.
Rank 04 — Reliable evidence
Curiosity-gap or insider knowledge text framing
"Pros do this", "Nobody teaches this", "But You Don't", "It Feels Illegal", "Sneaky techniques", "Secrets" — these text framings create an implied gap between what the viewer knows and what the creator knows. Appear consistently on mid-to-high performers across all three channels. Works best paired with a face expression that matches the framing — a slight smirk or knowing look, rather than a shocked wide-eyes expression.
Rank 05 — Contextual evidence
Famous creator name or brand prominently in thumbnail
Joey Edits' "Edit Like" series and Matt Loui's Vox/Ali Abdaal/Johnny Harris thumbnails consistently pull views. The famous creator's face or brand logo in the thumbnail borrows their search traffic and their audience's aspiration. Works especially well when that creator has a visually distinctive editing style (Vox, MagnatesMedia, Natt Jongsala). The thumbnail must make the connection obvious at a glance.
Rank 06 — Works when fresh
Timely AI tool pairing — "X + CapCut"
Joey Edits' "Claude + CapCut = GOD MODE" (14.3x) and "ChatGPT + CapCut = GOD MODE" (9.4x) show that pairing CapCut with a trending AI tool drives strong early momentum. The thumbnail for these uses the AI tool's logo + CapCut logo + face, which is clean and communicates the collaboration immediately. This format has a shorter shelf life than evergreen techniques but can spike hard when timed well.
What consistently does not work — evidenced across all three channels
  • Thumbnails with no face at all — software UI, app icons, or animation screenshots only. Across all three channels these are the bottom performers. No exceptions in the data.
  • Off-niche content regardless of thumbnail quality — MacBook reviews, DaVinci Resolve, fitness timers. The audience doesn't click these regardless of how good the thumbnail is.
  • Cluttered thumbnails with 3+ competing text elements or too many floating app icons — reduces focal clarity and hurts click-through.
  • Sponsored review content — UniConverter, Aiarty, Wondershare. Universally low performers across all three channels. Audiences can smell ads.
